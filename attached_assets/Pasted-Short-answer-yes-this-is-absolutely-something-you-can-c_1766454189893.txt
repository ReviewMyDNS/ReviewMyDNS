Short answer: yes, this is absolutely something you can continue with, and it has real potential. But it only makes sense to keep investing if you’re willing to (a) sharpen the positioning, and (b) commit to marketing/SEO, not just the code.

I’ll break this down into:

What’s good about ReviewMyDNS right now
The gaps / risks I see from the live site
When it’s worth continuing vs pivoting
What I’d do in the next 4–6 weeks before making a bigger decision
1. What’s good about ReviewMyDNS right now
From what I can see:

Clear core value: “Global DNS Propagation Checker” is simple and instantly understandable.
Tool surface area is strong:
Bulk DNS Lookup
DNS Comparison
Historical Tracking
Developer API
Performance Monitor
Security Check
DNS Analytics
That’s already more depth than many “single-tool” DNS sites.
Market exists and spends:
Agencies, MSPs, SaaS companies, infra teams, email deliverability geeks, etc. all care about:
DNS correctness
Email deliverability
SSL uptime
This is not a hobby problem; it ties directly to uptime and revenue.
Moat potential:
If you build history, monitoring, and smart recommendations (not just lookups), you’re moving from “utility” to “product.”
A freemium monitoring/analytics angle can differentiate you from basic one-off checkers.
So conceptually: this is not a dead idea. It’s a useful domain, and you’ve already shipped a real tool.

2. The main gaps / risks I see
Right now, from the outside:

Positioning is thin
Above-the-fold: a form plus “Global DNS Propagation Checker”.
Below: links to other tools and one “Sponsored: Professional DNS hosting” block.
There’s no clear story of:
Who this is for (MSPs? SaaS? domain owners? email folks?).
What makes it better than intoDNS, DNSChecker, MXToolbox, etc.
What you want users to do next beyond a single lookup.
Content is almost non-existent
No visible blog/guides/FAQ on the main page load I see.
Without content, organic traffic will stall and stay small.
You’re essentially competing as “just another DNS tool landing page,” and those incumbents have years of links.
No obvious “why to sign up”
If you’re aiming at revenue later (monitoring, history, API, etc.), there needs to be:
A visible concept of “account” or “dashboard”.
A reason to go from anonymous lookup → logged-in user (save domains, alerts, history).
Monetization not clear
I see a “Sponsored / Professional DNS Hosting” ad callout, which is fine, but:
No pricing page.
No plans or explanation of paid features.
Without revenue clarity, it’s hard to justify big time investment.
None of these are fatal. They’re “this is a strong prototype, not yet a business.”

3. Is this worth continuing?
It depends on two things:

Do you like this problem space (DNS, infra, email, uptime)?
If yes, then continuing makes sense. There’s enough money and enough unmet needs (especially around history, monitoring, change intelligence) that a motivated person can carve out a niche.
If no, this will feel like grinding on a low-visibility tool in a nerdy corner of the internet; you’ll lose steam.
Are you willing to do marketing + content, not just coding?
To go from ~1,000 uniques → 10k+ → 50k+, you’ll need:
Search-optimized pages
Guides
Community posts
Possibly partnerships
If you only want to hack on tools and never write or promote, growth will be slow and you might be better off treating this as a side project, not a main bet.
My honest recommendation, based on what’s built:

On a 1–10 “worth continuing as a serious project” scale, I’d put this at 7.5/10:
Strong enough concept and foundation.
Room for a differentiated product if you go beyond raw lookups.
But it requires deliberate marketing and positioning work.
If you’re okay with putting in 2–3 more focused months and then re-evaluating, I would absolutely continue.

4. What I’d do in the next 4–6 weeks before deciding longer term
Think of this as an experiment sprint. The goal: prove growth trajectory and validate usage, not to perfect everything.

4.1. Clarify who you’re for, and reflect that on the page
Pick 1–2 primary audiences to optimize around, for example:

“For DevOps and SRE teams managing production workloads”
Or “For agencies/MSPs managing many client domains”
Update the homepage with:

1–2 lines under the hero explaining:
Who it’s for
What problems it solves beyond “check propagation”
A small features strip:
“Monitor DNS changes over time”
“Compare providers before and after migrations”
“Get alerts when records drift”
4.2. Add one obvious step toward “product”, not just tool
Even a simple, free “account” with one strong benefit:

“Create a free account to:
Save up to X domains
View 30 days of DNS change history
Get weekly health summary”
You don’t need full billing yet. Just:

Signup/login
Domain list
Basic history (even if limited)
This lets you see if people want more than a one-off lookup.

4.3. Ship 3–5 high-value content pages
Very specifically:

Create /guides or /blog and write:
“DNS Propagation: How It Actually Works (and How to Check Properly)”
“Checklist: Moving DNS Providers Without Downtime”
“Diagnosing Email Delivery Problems with SPF, DKIM, and DMARC (Using ReviewMyDNS)”
“Common DNS Misconfigurations and How to Detect Them”
“How to Use DNS History to Debug Outages”
Each page:

Links directly to relevant tools.
Uses the same language as your users (“why is my site not updating”, “emails going to spam after DNS change”).
4.4. Make result pages shareable (at least for the propagation checker)
Implement:

A unique, copyable URL per lookup (domain + record type).
A visible “Copy result link” button.
Short human-readable summary at the top.
Then:

Start using those links yourself in any support/community answers you give.
This is the simplest way to see if organic sharing happens.
4.5. Do 2–3 distribution experiments
These are low-cost tests:

A “Show HN” or “Launch HN” post once UX/content is improved.
A Reddit post in r/sysadmin or r/devops that:
Explains a DNS troubleshooting flow.
Uses ReviewMyDNS as the visualizer.
One technical article on DEV.to or similar:
“How I built a global DNS checker with 50+ servers (and what I learned about DNS).”
Measure:

Traffic spike
Tool usage
Signups (if you add them)
When to not continue
You should consider pausing/changing direction if:

After a 2–3 month focused push (content + minor product polish + distribution), you see:
No signs of organic link growth.
Very low repeat usage (most users never come back).
Almost no one willing to create an account / sign up for history/monitoring.
And you personally:
Don’t enjoy DNS/infra enough to keep grinding on it.
Would rather be building something closer to your interests.
In that case, keep the site running as a small utility (it can still earn modest affiliate/sponsorship revenue over time), but move your main energy elsewhere.

From what I see, this is good enough to absolutely justify continuing, but:

As a focused experiment for the next couple of months.
With a mindset of: “Can I prove growing demand and repeat usage?”
